# Gary Stevenson — Bio

Gary Stevenson is a former trader turned economics educator, known as the "people's economist" and the creator of **Gary's Economics**, a YouTube channel focused on wealth inequality and how it affects ordinary people.

## Early life

Gary grew up in Ilford, a working-class area on the border of East London and Essex, in a tiny, narrow terraced house with his parents, older brother, and younger sister — five people in a small home. He shared a bunk bed with his brother in a converted bathroom-turned-bedroom. The main railway line from Essex into London ran right through the garden, past his bedroom window, and the whole house shook every time a fast train went through.

His family was poor. His dad worked his whole life earning around £20,000 a year, commuting from Seven Kings station into London every morning from 5:30am. Gary's earliest memory is of his mum waking the children up to wave at their dad as his train passed the house. Growing up, he owned just two pairs of shoes — both hand-me-downs from his brother.

There was no desk in the house to study at. Instead, his parents gave him a plank of wood, and he would lie belly-down on the floor of the front room doing his maths and French homework while his dad watched Coronation Street and his siblings ran around.

## Education

Gary attended a grammar school but was expelled at age 16 for selling drugs — kids from wealthier backgrounds had the money, and he had access to drugs on his street. After his expulsion, he buckled down and studied alone in the empty house every day, lying on the floor with his plank of wood. He achieved 8 A*s and 3 As in his GCSEs.

He went on to study maths and economics at the London School of Economics (LSE), where he was one of the top students. However, he found that grades alone weren't enough to land the top City jobs — those went to students with wealthy parents who had polished CVs full of extracurriculars. Gary's CV listed a paper round, working in a sofa shop, and trying to become a grime MC.

He found a back door: a trading card game competition run by a bank across five universities. He researched the rules beforehand, won the competition, and earned an internship. He was about 20 years old when he started interning at Citibank, waking up at 5am to shower with a stick-on hose in the bathtub before getting the train to Canary Wharf.

## Trading career

Gary became a trader at Citibank (now Citigroup). His job was to predict interest rates. After the 2008 financial crisis, he watched Lehman Brothers collapse and friends lose their jobs. He began copying successful traders around him, and in one year made $12 million in profit for the bank. His first big bonus was £395,000 — at age 23, having grown up skipping lunches and eating 75p scotch eggs from Tesco.

In 2010 he lost $8 million in a single week when the Swiss National Bank unexpectedly cut rates. This triggered an obsession with actually understanding how the economy works — something his university education and the trading floor had never really taught him. He came to the conclusion that wealth inequality was the fundamental problem, and that interest rates would stay low because the economy was structurally broken in ways economists kept failing to predict.

## Activism and media

Years after leaving trading, Gary started the Gary's Economics YouTube channel in 2020. His first video, published in July 2020, explained how COVID-19 money printing was making the rich richer while ordinary people's wages stagnated and house prices soared.

He wrote a book and has given lectures at places like Cambridge. He has a channel manager (Oscar) and a videographer (Jack), but creatively the channel is still essentially him. He lived in Japan for two years and has returned there multiple times.

His central message: if wealth inequality isn't addressed, life will keep getting worse for people from backgrounds like his. He advocates for taxing wealth rather than just income, arguing that national insurance rises tax ordinary workers while billionaires pay nothing. The voices dominating the economic conversation, he says, are paid for by billionaires — not representing ordinary people.
