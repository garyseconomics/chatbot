Yes, asymmetrical bets—where the potential upside far outweighs the risk—can be powerful tools for activists seeking systemic change. Drawing inspiration from the GameStop phenomenon, here are a few actionable strategies for average people to tilt the balance in favor of broader economic justice:

### 1. **Collective Information Sharing & Narrative Shifting**  
   Just as retail investors in GameStop used platforms like Reddit to coordinate, individuals can amplify truths about inequality through social media, grassroots campaigns, and community conversations. By consistently highlighting how policies or systems harm the majority (e.g., tax cuts for the wealthy, corporate subsidies), activists can shift public opinion. The "cost" is low (time, effort), but the "payoff" could be massive: policy changes, elections, or cultural shifts. For example, spreading awareness about how student debt or housing crises disproportionately affect working-class people could pressure lawmakers to act.

### 2. **Divestment & Ethical Consumerism**  
   Divesting from companies or industries that exacerbate inequality (e.g., fossil fuels, predatory lenders) or reinvesting in community-based cooperatives creates an asymmetrical bet. While individual divestment may seem small, collective action can weaken harmful systems and strengthen alternatives. Supporting local businesses or credit unions, for instance, bets on building resilient, equitable economic models. The risk? Minimal financial loss. The reward? A growing network of ethical alternatives that could scale into systemic change.

### 3. **Grassroots Political Mobilization**  
   Activists can "bet" on the power of numbers by organizing around specific issues (e.g., universal healthcare, rent control). By pooling resources to fund campaigns, endorse candidates, or run for office, individuals can create a ripple effect. The GameStop example showed how decentralized, coordinated action can disrupt markets; similarly, a unified push for progressive policies can disrupt the political status quo. The asymmetry here is that even modest local efforts can snowball into national movements if they resonate with the 90% who feel marginalized.

### 4. **Leveraging Legal & Regulatory Systems**  
   Supporting or initiating class-action lawsuits against corporations that engage in anti-competitive behavior (e.g., price-fixing, monopolies) is another asymmetrical bet. Legal victories can set precedents, redistribute wealth, and force accountability. For example, lawsuits against banks for predatory lending practices have led to billions in settlements for affected communities. The risk is minimal for participants, but the impact can be transformative.

### 5. **Crowdsourcing for Public Goods**  
   Platforms like Kickstarter or community bonds can be used to fund projects that address systemic issues, such as renewable energy, affordable housing, or public infrastructure. By pooling small contributions, individuals "bet" on the success of scalable solutions. If these projects gain traction, they could pressure governments to adopt similar models, creating a virtuous cycle of investment in the public good.

### 6. **Education as a Weapon**  
   Creating accessible content (videos, podcasts, infographics) that simplifies complex economic issues (e.g., how tax policies favor the wealthy) can be a low-risk, high-impact bet. By making inequality a "new common sense," as Gary emphasizes, activists can normalize demands for change. The GameStop event succeeded in part because it was framed in terms of fairness and corporate accountability—something relatable to the masses.

### Why This Works  
Asymmetrical bets in activism thrive on **united, decentralized action**. When millions of people take small, coordinated steps (e.g., voting, sharing a post, divesting), the cumulative effect can destabilize entrenched power structures. The key is to align individual actions with broader movements, ensuring that the "bet" is not just personal but part of a collective push for systemic change. After all, as Gary’s work shows, the current system is structurally skewed—so even modest efforts can tip the scales if enough people act in unison.
